Journal

2026 is the year of the Italian wedding. Between my friend Jake’s Tuscan wedding and my own wedding being located in Lake Garda, my free time for backpacking trips has been squeezed. The most significant consequence of this compressed schedule is that I had one real shot at completing a longer section hike this year, and the Arizona Trail was the lucky winner of the sweepstakes.

Taking a step back, let’s dive into how I arrived at the Arizona Trail, both literally and figuratively. When Jake told us that his bachelor’s party was going to be located in the desert southwest in April, my ears perked up and immediately told the brain to start planning a desert hike. The question then became, where? The PCT in Southern California, a section of the Hayduke, the Zion-Traverse, a section of the Arizona Trail, all presented themselves from the jump. In fact, options seemed to sprout out of every corner of my consciousness, but slowly the logistical burden of various options crafted a realistic pecking order of the most likely trips. The option that floated to the top? A SOBO section of the AZT starting in Pine, AZ.

Something like 130 words does not do the amount of gymnastics required to pull off the logistics justice, but the reality is that searching for rental car drop-off points in the desert isn’t a very exciting task. But there it was, like an oasis, the Payson Automotive Collision Center, my humble drop-off point. With a start line picked out, I needed a finish line. Luckily for me, the Arizona Trail only has two options: north or south. Sometimes an equation can look relatively easy upon first glance, only for variables to add a sprinkle of complexity. Our show-stealers of variables that occupied my mind for months were 1) the desert heat and 2) water scarcity due to a low snow year. Thus, the permutations began, and my notebook soon became adorned with the scriblings of a madman. If I went north, I’d avoid the heat, but the water might be harder to find as snowmelt is more important along this stretch of trail. Add to that the logistics of getting to the airport would be far more challenging. If I went south, I risked getting cooked alive in the Sonoran Desert and seeing water sources evaporate before my eyes, but I’d be able to get to the Phoenix airport if I did make it to the end. Two pages of paper full of historical weather data, mileage points, and potential water carries later, I decided that I was overthinking and walked away. The logic: a lot of people who are far dumber than I am end up pulling this off every year, and working myself into a pretzel of indecision wasn’t worth it.

What did I do with that time instead? Strength training, mobility work, and gear manufacturing. I had a few months to prep in front of me and decided to spend a lot of time focusing on getting my body as strong as possible, and my gear dialed in. I bought a heavier kettlebell, started doing a structured weight training plan, consistently did rehab exercises for the various aches and pains in my body, and made pieces of gear that were both light and functional. Week by week, I noticed my body getting stronger and more mobile. The upgrades in gear and the choices to cut items from the gear list cut down the weight that’d be on my back. And my mind was squarely focused on making this trip a success. Despite a cold, dark winter, I put myself in a position to succeed long before I hit the trail. I may have had a lingering issue or two, but I was certain that I had done as much as I could to prepare for the section hike. As the hike got closer and the forecast came into view, I made a final decision to go south, come hell or high water.

With the logistics nailed down and the body in near-peak condition, I decided it was time to stop thinking about the backpacking trip and focus on my friend’s bachelor trip. My feet hit the ground in Las Vegas, Nevada, at 1 am local time on Thursday morning, rocking a bag built for a female in her mid-20s that held all of my bachelor’s party clothing, while also sporting a handmade backpacking pack with poles showing off that I was there for business, too. Here is a good time to add that traversing the floor of an upscale casino at 1:30 am on a weekday with a backpacking kit on your back is a strange experience. Remember that I had just worked a full day, flown six hours across the country, and now slots rang out all around me. My brain was about the same consistency as mashed potatoes by the time I made it to the elevator bank. One sheep… two sheep…three shee……. down for the night.

Just five hours later, my watch blared and alerted me that I had a meeting in 20 minutes. With my buddy slowly rising next to me, I adorned my golf polo and set off to the business center to find a space for a call. Lo and behold, the business center was closed, so I found a quiet couch and posted up for my final meeting of the week. Luckily, I had a slower work day and got the opportunity to squeeze in one final lower-body strength training session at lunchtime. The workday eventually came to a close, and the side of my life dedicated to making a living was put on hold for a while as it was now time to focus my attention and energy squarely on my friend.

And that is exactly what I did: the laptop was powered off, the phone was tucked away, and my presence was exactly where it needed to be. Our group spent the first night together in Vegas, and we kicked off the festivities with a pregame in the penthouse suite. Sticking to the bougie theme, Nobu was the destination for dinner, and the luxe casino floors were our landing spot for games late into the night. After far too few hours of sleep, we drove out to St. George, Utah, and immediately flung ourselves into Utility Terrain Vehicles, where we drove up sand dunes and rocky mountain sides. The rest of that night was a mixture of pizza and beer with a dash of hot tub. Saturday rolled around, and we donned our finest polos and liters of sunscreen for the five hours of golf that lay ahead. Despite being incredibly rusty, I shot an 84 and came fourth out of eleven, respectable performance. Things spiced up after the golf as an order-in hibachi chef brought the energy (and unlimited sake). A night of debauchery later, the sun rose on Sunday morning, and the bachelor party had come to a close.

The next morning was spent saying goodbye to everybody before making my way to the next leg of my journey. My friends Cam and Eric dropped me off in St. George so that I could knock out a few logistical hurdles. A concoction of Target, Firehouse Subs, FedEx, and Enterprise left me with enough water to get to and start the trail, a full stomach, snacks for the road, a lightened load with all of my work/bachelor party items sent to New York, and a Nissan Sentra. I was off.

The open road stretched out in front of me, and the South Rim of the Grand Canyon was stop numero uno on the itinerary. I was expecting a fairly non-descript drive through a barren wasteland of desert prior to climbing up to the canyon rim. I couldn’t have been more wrong. The drive from St. George to the Grand Canyon was one of the most beautiful 5-hour stretches of highway driving that I have done in my life. Giant red cliffs rising near Hurricane, Utah scrambled my East Coast brain, endless deserts drove my mind to the edge thinking of the vastness of the world we inhabit, the pine forests rising around Jacob Lake shocked the system with the stark contrast from the world you were in only minutes ago, the Vermilion cliffs shot like knives towards the blue skies above, the Colorado River raged below at Marble Canyon, and all of a sudden I turned west and saw a sign noting that the park entrance is only 27 miles away. This may seem a bit silly, but I told myself that I wanted my first view of the Grand Canyon to be on my feet, not through the automobile, so I put my head down and avoided the views on the road and motored on to the parking lot near Mather Point. Out $35 and 20 minutes after passing the toll booth, I pulled into the lot and made my way to the edge of the rim on foot. As I approached the edge of the canyon, I noticed that everything was very manicured and everybody was on their respective devices. I tried to shake it off, but upon reaching Mather Point, I lifted my head to take in the view for the first time, and I was… underwhelmed. For my entire life, I had been told that seeing the Grand Canyon is such an unbelievable experience and that for many, it is emotional and even spiritual. I did not have that experience. Sure, the view was incredible, and I had never seen anything like it before, but the throng of tourists with their selfie sticks and the lack of effort to get there left a sour taste in my mouth. I tried to block out the tourists and take in the experience, but I felt the pull to get out of there. So I did. I jumped in the car and beelined for the South Kaibab trail.

The National Park Service has to manage visitor traffic in the busy seasons, and because of this, the parking lot at the trailhead was off-limits to personal vehicles. So, I found where the Arizona Trail intersects with the Rim Road and (legally) parked on the shoulder. I had a short walk through the woods, and after a few minutes, I got a more organic view of the canyon. No structure overhanging the edge. No selfie sticks. Just a rocky outcropping that has seen far too many footprints. Although you can only make one first impression, my experience with the Grand Canyon had already begun rewriting the first sour taste in my mouth. A few minutes later, I descended into the canyon.

An easy way to approach talking about the Grand Canyon is to go on about its immense scale or beauty, but I think that it's a bit of a cop-out. To me, this place signifies the duality of our world. As you stand on top of the rim, you are exposed to modern society, development for the sake of development, and overcrowding. There isn’t much that is too different from the rest of your life, other than you are looking at a living piece of art. The natural world isn’t a living and ever-evolving thing in this sphere; it is something to be seen, a box to be checked. Not to be experienced or lived. Below the rim, and more specifically, the further you descend into the canyon, this paradigm shifts. Modernity fades away, and you start to experience the place, you feel the time, you feel the elevation, you feel the change. And with thinking about the canyonlands in the desert Southwest, there is no person better suited to describe what I’m getting at than Edward Abbey when he says, “Men come and go, cities rise and fall, whole civilizations appear and disappear - the Earth remains, slightly modified. The Earth remains, and the heartbreaking beauty where there are no hearts to break....I sometimes choose to think, no doubt perversely, that man is a dream, thought an illusion, and only rock is real. Rock and sun.”

I barely scratched the surface of the Grand Canyon. In fact, I only descended a few thousand feet below the rim. So believe me, I know I am not in a position to talk about the Grand Canyon at length, but I did feel the shift below the rim. Because of the time of day, the hikers mostly petered out as the light faded, leaving me with a trail to myself to go back up to the rim. As the sun set, I gawked at the expanding shadows stretching out in the canyon beneath me, and before I knew it, I only had a few hundred feet of climbing remaining. Ten minutes before sunset, I ran into a bighorn sheep on trail, and we got to see each other. We stood suspended in one another’s worlds for probably thirty seconds or so before carrying forward in our separate directions. The sheep descended into the canyon, and I returned to modernity up above. I walked through my favorite part of the day, dusk, and got back to my car right before a headlamp was necessary. As I climbed into the car and plotted the route ahead, my thoughts returned to the bighorn sheep, and a pang of jealousy hit me that, despite all of my comfort and certainty in life, the bighorn sheep is living more closely to how life was intended to be lived than I. Ouch. The accelerator was pressed down, and Flagstaff lay ahead.

At this point in the journey, I was fully winging it. The only real responsibility was to be at the rental car counter in Payson at 8 am the following morning. So, with my mind on autopilot while thinking about the bighorn sheep, I got gas, ate dinner, and blew right through Flagstaff. Before I knew it, I pulled over on some random Forest Service road in the Coconino National Forest between Flagstaff and Pine and called the flat patch home for the night. As I was doing my final nighttime chores, I looked up at the night sky above and saw a sky full of stars. I stood suspended in the silence and beauty of this northern Arizona forest, and I considered throwing my sleeping mat on the dirt next to the rental car, but instead I rolled down the windows and slept in the car. After my beauty rest came to a close, the only thing left was to close out the road trip by returning the rental car in Payson and finding my way to trail in Pine.

That morning, I closed out the drive and made my way into Payson, where I filled up the car with gas and ate one of the larger breakfasts of my life at Crosswinds, right next to the local airstrip. After sufficiently stuffing myself, I became part of the queue at the Payson Enterprise drop-off at 7:45 am and twiddled my thumbs waiting to get out of there. Before I knew it, the Uber that I called to whisk me to Pine arrived only to reveal a flat tire. The man was nice enough to drive me to a busier road crossing, where I stuck out my thumb and fished for a ride north. After a slight repositioning fifteen minutes into the fishing expedition, the line tugged, and a four-door sedan came to a halt about fifty yards past me. I climbed into the car with a young couple who were travelling the country together in their car, and we got talking about our separate lives before it came out that we had the same favorite band. The windows were promptly rolled down, and a delightful backseat karaoke session carried me twenty minutes up the road to Pine. Now, did they drop me off in the right spot? Of course not. Did I have to bushwhack a little bit to get to the start of trail? Of course. Was it worth it? You bet.

9:02 am on Monday, April 20th, is when I officially stepped onto the Arizona Trail (“AZT”), meaning that the foot travel portion of the journey, and the reason for this write-up, had officially begun.

My first steps on trail were in an open, lush pine forest. The air felt crisp, life was in every nook and cranny. Immediately, I was caught by surprise. Maybe it’s because I had envisioned my time on the AZT as one long desert hike, or maybe because I hadn’t seen anything like it previously. Whatever it was, my mind was captivated by this unique ecosystem. As my mind was busy taking in all the new senses, like a curious toddler, I almost physically ran into the first thru-hiker I saw on trail. As is a common theme throughout this trip, it was not long before saying farewell to the first hiker that another seemed to spring out of the ground. This fine individual was a triple crowner, meaning he had hiked all three major long-distance trails in the United States (Appalachian Trail, Pacific Crest Trail, and Continental Divide Trail), and had hiked the AZT before. I got talking to him near the first water source of the day, and it turns out the reason he came back out to the Arizona Trail was that he realized that he had no memory of the Matazatal Wilderness Area. So, he wanted to go back through the mountain range and walk it more slowly this time, taking in the experience by appreciating the land that he was passing through, instead of simply racing through. A beautiful, intentional endeavor. His approach immediately rubbed off on me. Meeting this person so early in the trip was a blessing because it offered a pause in my frenetic mind to recognize the power of the present moment that I’d be experiencing throughout the journey. And even though I had to maintain high mileage days to meet my logistical itinerary, I could still carry his lesson with me as the days slowly ticked by.

The middle portion of day one was marked by exposed terrain that was generally flat or downhill as the trail rode ridgelines and the sides of mountains on the descents. As elevation slowly decreased on the march to the East Verde River, the tree canopy shrank and cacti and shrubbery took their rightful place. It wasn’t long before I officially entered the Mazatzal Wilderness Area, and the unfamiliar arid environment had taken hold. This was my first true dose of desert hiking. The dust, the water uncertainty, and the unrelenting sun were to be my closest companions over the next week. But for now, it was new and fresh. Another aspect of the Arizona Trail that I hadn’t expected was how expansive the views would be in every direction, and day one came out swinging with the wall of the Mazatzal Mountains inching closer hour by hour as I clung to the ridgelines. As the afternoon angled the beating sun onto my pasty, white skin, I began my descent to the river in earnest by dropping down into Rock Canyon. After a few hours, I moseyed on up to the East Verde River, popped my shirt off, and dunked my feet into an inlet stream while I tended to a few chores. The shade and the sound of running water threatened to throw me into a trance-like state where I was unable to break its grasp and continue. The event that broke the trance? Let’s call it incredibly loud splashing behind me. At first, I thought there was another hiker who came up behind me unknowingly and was poking fun at me for taking a little catnap. Oh boy, was I wrong. How about a bull? Like yours truly, a giant bovine had decided that the East Verde River was a perfect afternoon getaway and wanted to splash around a little. I lifted the cap from covering my eyes, sat up a bit, took note of the bull, and promptly resumed my nap.

Despite being at twenty miles for the day, I decided that I should press on and shouldered the pack once more. The walk along the East Verde River was lovely. Lush. Easy trail. What more could a guy ask for? Well, for starters, for that to be the official part of the Arizona Trail. Turns out, I missed a turn and had followed a cattle track for half a mile. The landmines of dung served as a warning for my little peanut brain that something might be off, and boy was I right. So, I then commenced part two of bushwhacking for the first day. I fought through sandy washes, vertical walls to get out of said washes (that tested my rock climbing abilities), and even had a brief foray with barbed wire. At some point, I exited the mental and physical quagmire and found myself at the base of the climb that served as the spiritual gateway into the Mazatzal Mountains.

Now might be a good time to mention that I was carrying all of the food for the entire trip on my back. Let’s call it six and a half days of food, hitting the scales at eleven pounds. With two liters of water and my dinner reconstituting in the peanut butter jar, this probably touches five pounds of water weight. And to top it all off, my gear is roughly eight pounds. Breaking out the calculator, we have about twenty-four pounds loaded up on my back at this point. Here is where you can pile on and tell me to cry you a river, but I have another point that may stop you from doing so. Ah-hem. At some point in time, I believed that a pack without a hipbelt would solve all my problems. I’d become fabulously wealthy, more powerful than anybody could imagine, and my base weight would look delicious in a spreadsheet. Turns out that I don’t really care about being wealthy or powerful, but the spreadsheet weight is quite delicious. The reality is more in line with your trapezius and shoulder muscles being extremely tired when you’re eight hours into the day (I’m not shit-talking the pack either, it actually was my favorite pack I’ve ever used). So, with a scrambled brain, frustrated from a wrong turn and sore shoulders, I kicked it into low gear and began the ascent.

As I motored up the first hill, I said goodbye to the East Verde River and the last man-made building (or road) I’d pass for roughly fifty miles. The climb itself towered above with over 2,600 feet of vertical gain in around four and a half miles. I set my timer on my watch for an hour and told myself to ascend until it rang. At that point, I’d throw my gear down and eat dinner. Before too long, the hour of climbing passed, and the watch rang, but I was too deep in the groove at that point, so I carried on. It wasn’t until an irresistible spot called for me to take a load off and enjoy dinner with a view and a cool breeze. There is something about that first meal on trail that hits the spot, even if you are a dirty cold-soaker like me. Somehow, a mix of barley, refried beans, taco seasoning, and corn chips all soaked in cold water can taste good when you have views this pristine. With a full belly and thirty minutes of climbing remaining, I clicked into the familiar low gear and worked my way to the top of the climb. As I neared the saddle that marked the high point for the climb, I noticed something large moving on the ground near me. The camouflaged creature darted on the trail just a few feet in front of me, and I stopped to observe something I had never seen in the wild before: a tarantula! At about the size of my fist, the caricatures of this incredible spider lived up to the hype, and I took a moment just watching him in his natural habitat before snapping a picture and carrying on. The little critter gave me all the energy I needed to finish the climb, and before I knew it, I had arrived at the gateway that would expose the interior beauty of the Mazatzal Mountains.

I thought there should’ve been some harps playing as I rounded the bend, but the universe wasn’t totally in sync with me at that moment. I’ll chalk it up to it having more pressing needs. However, I was greeted by one of the most beautiful sights of untouched wilderness I had ever seen, so we’ll call it even. All I could see in every direction was a sea of mountains, covered in green, with the angling light from the sun casting shadows on the hills near and far. And unlike the East Coast, I had these views for miles, not just a temporary overlook. So, my last few miles of the day were filled with awe and beauty as the last of the daylight exited the stage. Shortly after sunset, I hit the icon that marked water on the FarOut guide that signaled the end of my day. No water. My desert-virgin brain went into overdrive. How am I going to do without water for the night? Should I keep going? Should I bushwhack downstream? Then, I opened the guide, realized the water was 0.1 miles further down, and walked to it. Brush Springs did, in fact, exist. As I was filling up my bottles, a hiker named Speedy came up to the source, and we started chatting. Turns out he is a triple-crowner, and he was already 34 miles into the day and had been on the trail for less than two weeks since starting at the Mexican border. Instead of stopping at the nice site where I had thrown down my gear, he donned the headlamp and continued into the night. Maybe he didn’t like me, maybe he wanted more miles? Maybe he likes night hiking? Who knows. Speedy is an enigma. One thing that was very real about Speedy, he had the most thrashed shoes that I have ever seen. Godspeed, Speedy.

The stars began illuminating above as I finished my stretching routine and climbed into the bug bivy. I gave a low-energy recap on the day via voice memo and let the tight grove of trees lining the river caress me into a deep sleep. So, that brings day one to a close. The stats for the day: 27.6 miles (1.0 off-trail); total time: 10:06:23; moving time: 8:53:05; and elevation gain: 4,513 ft.

The watch chimed at 5:15 am and then began the eternal battle of sleep and snooze. Eventually, the watch won out, and I was packing up and starting my morning chores. With the chores in the rear view mirror, I began my second favorite part of the day, the early morning miles when the world is waking up alongside me (only beaten out by dusk). The morning of day two was spent meandering through a lush pine forest that felt a world away from the popular perception of Arizona. My little bubble of solitude was popped a bit after mile three on the day when I ran into two women who were packing up camp. Here is a good point to interject and mention that it wasn’t the sight of the other hikers that threw off the sense of solitude, as that often endures through the short conversations, rather it was the mention that there was heavy mountain lion activity in the area the previous night. I didn’t let it get under my skin too badly, but there aren’t many critters that have the mix of stealth and lethality that makes the hair stand up on your neck like a mountain lion. Sprinkle on the fact that I was deep in a wilderness area and far from the nearest road or exit point. It makes it a little bit more nervy than you’d like. Needless to say, my pace quickened a bit through that section.

Because mountain lions obviously stay in one place and never roam (~sarcasm~), once I was a mile beyond the water source, mountain lions were simply just a figment of my imagination, just as before. That freedom of thought and anxiety allowed me to see the land for its beauty, rather than its danger, once again. The lush pine forests, the rolling green terrain, and the interspersed ridges and saddles kept the energy high all morning. If the bubble of solitude wasn’t penetrated by the two women and the mountain lion knowledge earlier in the morning, it certainly was popped by the conga line of northbound thru-hikers that I passed starting as the dawn transitioned to the late morning. The first two of the bunch were Paiute, an older gentlemen who had thru-hiked the PCT, and Kevin. I met them at the base of one of the bigger climbs of the day, and we stopped and talked for about five or ten minutes. When in a two-on-one conversation on trail, I have found that you gravitate towards talking to the first person of the pair much more. Anyways, Paiute was the first of the two that I ran into, and his calm aura immediately appeared. The way he carried himself felt so foreign relative to the typical attitudes and actions of the city-dwellers and suburbanites of the East. The conversation wrapped up, and they pointed out the saddle that I was shooting for, and away I went. Just shy of 7,000 feet of elevation gain, I reached the top of the climb and continued down the trail until I reached the junction with Hopi Spring. In a shocking turn of events, I found myself wading through the mud and rock-hopping as I approached a spring catchment box that was overflowing. I refilled my bottles, took a little break, and after shaking out the debris in my shoes, continued south on the AZT.

It wasn’t long before the conga line picked up its intensity. Wild Child, a Vermontean thru-hiker who oozed coolness, was the first hiker I passed. We traded information on upcoming water sources and carried on our respective journeys. Within an hour, I was trailside at a small pool chatting up a hiker named Handlebar, who sported one hell of a mustache. He is currently residing in Flagstaff and told me about how much it has changed since the “boom” in 2019. I commented on how the Hudson Valley is going through something similar. He abruptly cut in and asked where in the Hudson Valley. When I explained that I lived in Cold Spring, he mentioned that his gig after the AZT was a summer camp based in Copake Falls. We shared stories about the Hudson Valley, welcomed two older section hikers heading north, and wished each other well.

The trail carried on below Mazatzal Peak, and the bare rock faces rising above rivaled those that I saw in the Dolomites last summer, just a little bit more red. My relationship with this mountain was similar to that of Copper Mountain on the Colorado Trail. It began far off in the distance at least a full day prior to arriving, and although it would hide behind a ridge for a little while, it would inevitably reemerge and remain a staple of the horizon, just a little closer each time. By the time that I was walking on the mountain, I came up with what would eventually become the tagline of the trip: “With persistence, a continued belief in your abilities, and adherence to your broader purpose, the result is inevitable.” Being in these wild, raw places allows me to tap into corners of my brain that I don’t typically use on a day-to-day basis. When I started thinking about reaching this mountain and how it was on my mind for days, I started to think about all of the moments in my life where I have fully committed myself to something, and a funny pattern emerged. When I have the three attributes mentioned above (persistence, a rock-solid belief in myself, and a north star worth pursuing), I have always reached my goal. Maybe the bar isn’t being set high enough, or I’m showing off selection bias, but I firmly believe that I am an extremely high achiever in those circumstances. Anyways, bringing us back on trail after our quick detour, I knew at this point, I was finishing the AZT section hike, it was inevitable. Before I got too cocky and started hyping myself up too much, I ran into another thru-hiker, who was sporting a baguette popping out of the back of his pack. When I mentioned that this was my first time hiking in Arizona, he affectionately welcomed me to “the frying pan.” Unfortunately, I can’t remember this fine fella’s name, but he left me with a real gem. He said, “I’ve been staring at this shadow being cast by the baguette for hours, and if I don’t eat it soon, I’m going to throw myself off this mountain.” Farewell, Mr. Baguette, and may you find everything you knead.

Although I didn’t go to the summit of Mazatzal Peak, there was a climb that brought me higher, and right at the tippy top, I met two more thru-hikers, this time from Ottawa. Again, I focused my attention on the first hiker I saw, Matt, and didn’t say too much to the other guy, whose name I promptly forgot. Here is a good moment to describe my appearance on trail, I looked like a dumbass. My fit is a button-down collared shirt, khaki-colored hiking pants, a sun cape to cover my neck from the mean sun, and hand covers with open palms. I look like a middle school science teacher met a small Korean woman who loves golfing. I interrupted the regularly scheduled programming to say this because I feel extremely dumb when talking to other people my age. Without having to come across too strongly, I have to convince my fellow hikers that, despite looking like a total dork, I am actually “one of the boys” and am quite cool (~I don’t actually think or do this~). So anyways, those guys probably left thinking look at this dork in the desert, and you know what, I don’t blame them. Au revoir, my friends from the Great North.

On the other side of Mazatzal Peak, the views opened up to show off the lush pine forest in the foreground and the unforgiving desert peaks far off in the distance. The rest of the early afternoon was drop-dead gorgeous. The mountainous terrain of the Mazatzal Wilderness Area was just showing off at this point, with tall pine forests coating the mountain sides, but still offered enough space to get the expansive views that I had come to love throughout this range. After a liter of water being my companion for six miles, it was time to down it and find some more. So, I took a detour off trail to Bear Creek where I gathered water out of a hole in the ground, not even exaggerating, while a family of bees sang to me. A comment in the guidebook told me that if I was nice to the bees, they wouldn’t sting, and I can confirm that the guide was correct. With the hole water in my bottles, the journey continued. The trail rode the edges of the mountains for the remainder of the afternoon as I remained in the gravitational pull of Mazatzal Peak. At a moment in time when I was deep in my head, immersed in the thought of the hour, I turned a bend and almost crashed into two hikers. After quite literally having a jump scare, I apologized if my squeal alarmed them (~for the record, I did not squeal - sarcasm is difficult to convey via text - I don’t want you thinking I’m a dork like the two Canadian bros~). The two hikers were a husband and wife who have bikepacked and hiked the Arizona Trail and were now hiking it in sections. Our conversations rambled on, and before I knew it, fifteen minutes had passed. Ending a conversation with people you enjoy talking to isn’t always the easiest task, because you know you’ll likely never see them again. Other times, there is a clear conversation ender. This time, the conversation ender was two fighter jets from the U.S. military darting through the open, blue skies. A sonic boom cut off the conversation immediately. We all stared at the planes as the flew towards the horizon. “Is this normal?” “Yep. Happens all the time.” “Pretty amazing. We’ll anyways, have a great hike, and it was great talking to you.” And they shifted from the present to the past just like that.

After leaving the section hikers, my math brain activated and started going over just how long it took for those fighter jets to cover the distance that had taken me over two days to walk. The result: somewhere around 20-30 seconds to go 50 miles. What a clear way to call out the futility of the journey. Here’s the thing, I don’t really care that others find what I do to be a waste of time, a waste of energy, a waste of lifeforce, etc. What those cretens will never understand is the enrichment of my mind, body, and soul that is only possible when out in these settings. To those who may judge me for how I decide to spend my time and energy, the message is clear, “shove it :)”.

With a belly full of grub, I started the most beautiful hour of walking on the entire trip. The mountains became more jagged in the foreground, and the background was layered with mountains carrying on as far as the eye could see. The greenery was overwhelming nearby, but the contrast was readily available with the desert easily identifiable off in the distance. Mazatzal Peak is a greedy little bugger; it demands your full attention throughout this stretch. Luckily, it rewards you with incredible vistas of red cliffs rising to the blue skies above. I wasn’t done seeing hikers. One came shooting through, like a bullet, threatening to go straight through me if I didn’t cede the trail. He was obviously in the “mile crusher” mode, so I nodded his way, and we carried on. Just after this interaction, I crossed the Mazatzal Wilderness Area border and bid farewell until next time. A few campsites called after me with their sweet siren songs, offering beautiful vistas, but unfortunately, I was low on water and needed to press on. I descended the mountain and came up to a strategically placed water cache where I filled a few bottles. There, I met yet another hiker, and his outfit was a nice mix of Oscar the Grouch and the Cookie Monster. My man strategically positioned his site for the night to be at the lowest-effort water humanely possible on trail, respect. I descended another mile and made it to my little patch of heaven for the evening.

The final two hikers of the day were Atlas, a self-proclaimed geography nerd, and Briar, a curling aficionado. Atlas is the first time thru-hiker who is working for the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service. He grew up in Knoxville, Tennessee, and, like yours truly, is a Braves fan. Aside from his hiking endeavors, he is on a mission to visit every county in the United States. Needless to say, I was in the company of a psychopath. Briar is from Alberta, Canada, and thru-hiked the Appalachian Trail in 2022. He dealt with some injury complications after his AT thru-hike, and the Arizona Trail is the first thru-hike that he’s attempted since the Appalachian Trail. All of us sat around long past midnight (9 pm), chatting about life and joking around. As many of the overnight camping conversations with other long-distance hikers go, the topic inevitably circled around to the fact that I did not have a trail name. I told the tales of trail names that other folks tried to give me, Fleet Feet and Cougar Bait, but explained that they were either too self-congratulatory or inappropriate for my taste. At one point, Atlas wanted to make it something finance-related, so I promptly derailed the creative brainstorming session in an effort to keep that part of my life as far away from the trail as possible. That conversation was put on the back burner for a while as we bounced from topic to topic, joke to joke. Don’t ask me how, but Heated Rivalry, an HBO show about two NHL stars falling in love, came up (probably because Briar was our resident hockey expert of the evening). I explained how my experience of the show was listening to it, instead of watching, while trying to play cards with my friends, as the girls took it all in on the nearby couch. If you aren’t familiar with the show, it is extremely explicit with the audio just as intense as the visuals. I described trying to make a strategic decision in a card game while listening to a man moan over your shoulder is quite difficult. Right then, Briar suggested a new trail name for me, “Homophobic.” He described how the perfect introduction on trail would sound like, “Hi there, I’m Homophobic! How are you?” We sat in the dark with one another, absolutely belly-laughing at this revelation.

After more laughs and stories shared, we turned into our sleeping bags for the night and bid one another farewell. Although it was never serious and all for a good laugh, for a few minutes, I was Homophobic. So, that brings day two to a close. The stats for the day: 30.3 miles (1.0 off-trail); total time: 12:39:09; moving time: 10:12:00; and elevation gain: 5,888 ft.

The internal alarm clock had been set to 5:15 am, and on cue, I found myself rising before the sun. Atlas and Briar were not stirring yet, so I quietly packed my things and started my day. The first portion of the day was descending down brushy hill before hitting a water source where the trail quickly turned into a gradual climb. Enter the first problem of day three: I had to go to the bathroom, and there was nowhere to do so. Just shrubs and shrubs for miles. Mercifully, I found the slightest sliver of flat, non-shrubby ground and ended the suffering. Not long after, I reentered the final six-mile stretch of the Mazatzal Wilderness Area. During my final two hours, I reminisced on my experience through the Mazatzals and just how wild and wonderful the area felt. This final stretch was not as grand as the heart of the Mazatzal range, but it did serve as a point where I could reflect on the experience that I was currently immersed in, and that alone was worth the price of admission (~free~).

Once I exited the wilderness area, I truly entered the desert terrain that I had built up Arizona to be. I crossed a bunch of land dedicated to cattle grazing and felt like I was continuously opening and closing gates. As I passed through this section, there was a simple goal in mind: get to the highway at mile sixteen on the day, and reassess the simple goal for the day. After exiting the wilderness area, the trail was almost exclusively downhill, so the mind could float to whatever space it wanted to without being sidetracked by exertion. Where did my consciousness float to? Well, nowhere. Boredom, maybe. How often do I actually experience boredom in my daily life? Very little. Why do I seem so hellbent on avoiding boredom? I don’t have a good reason. Maybe because it is uncomfortable. Then, I was struck with a thought of the power of boredom. The state of boredom promotes creativity, introspection, and patience. Mainstream society pokes at you whenever you feel bored, to either reach for your device of choice or to be productive in some money-making endeavor. But why are we being told not to be bored, and why do we accept it willingly? Why not sit by yourself for a bit and see what you think when you remove the external noise?

As I was contemplating the role of boredom in modern life, the train of thought was derailed when I smelled laundry detergent coming down the trail a bit ahead. Once again, another thru-hiker, this time surprisingly clean, named Porcupine. She was a really lovely individual and had the chops that I had come to expect when meeting other hikers. She has done many primo hikes, including the PCT, Colorado Trail, Kungsladen, and a handful of other smaller trails. Fresh off a double zero, she did not sport the typical hiking funk, but the smell of Tide and scent boosters still threw my senses into a tizzy. Good time to interject, when you are away from the “real world” for a few days, your sight, taste, and smell all sharpen and take on lives of their own. This is why I could smell her before I could see her. Anyways, we traded notes on water, and I wished her well on her journey to Utah. It wasn’t long before I stumbled into the next thru-hikers, Pineapple Bitch and Chickweed. The aura of these two was different than Porcupine, the clean-cut hiker. Dreadlocks, tattoos, piercings, and a carnivore diet were the markers of these two free souls. One of the best parts of trail is the fact that you talk to people that you very rarely would interact with in daily life, and it is often these interactions that invite you to see the world differently. We stopped on trail and talked for maybe ten minutes, and covered a lot of our respective life paths. Chickweed grew up in Boston and currently lives in Atlanta, and at one point in her life worked a corporate job before swearing it off forever and growing out her dreadlocks. Pineapple Bitch, named after the pineapple-like lump of dreadlocks on top of his head, was a quieter guy from Germany who was immersing himself in the American long-distance backpacking culture. They are former vegetarians that have now morphed to full-on carnivores, and rumor has it that their food bag is full of beef tallow, olive oil, and jerky. Long way of saying, free thinkers that are carving their own groove through life. Anyways, as we were saying goodbye, I let them know that I was simply “Patrick” and I did not have a trail name. Chickweed looks at me and lets me know that she is going to call me “Ghost.” An ode to my pasty white skin, my ghost like desert fit, or going against the flow of traffic as a SOBO? Who knows. What is known, Ghost was born in the shadow of Arizona Highway 87. It wasn’t long after that that I got to try on the name for the first time. Another thru-hiker passed by as I was resting in the culvert underneath the highway. “Hi, I’m Ghost.”

Lovingly, of course, I thought of this area of trail to be a “connector” section, one whose purpose is delivering hikers from one notable area to another. And as airline travelers know all too well, connections are often where problems arise. The problem du joir was blisters. All day, the socks and shoes came off, and each time it felt like a new blister had sprouted up. After spending all winter in the cold, the skin on my feet had not had to deal with the heat and friction and had grown soft. Throw in a whole lot of heat and a boatload of friction with all the dirt and dust, and my feet stood no chance, especially as I got into terrain that was more deserty. A welcome relief came just a few miles after the highway crossing with Sycamore Creek, a gently flowing, cold water source that begged for my feet to get a nice bath. So, that’s exactly what I did, and while I was there, I got to talk to a day hiker from the local area. We shared stories, he tried to give me his magical cooling towel, he tried to give me some extra food, he tried to give me extra water, etc. A generous fella, but my feet were feeling good, and if I sat any longer, it was going to be nap time. In a ritual that I was getting quite good at due to all the experience, I said goodbye and carried on.

Although the next part of the day was a 2,800-foot ascent over six miles, the real battle was mental. The trail wove in and out of Boulder Creek for the first portion of the climb, but the water was long gone and the exposed terrain allowed for the sun to beat down and pump up the heat. Adding to this, the trail was a bit overgrown, and small spiky plants were constantly finding their way into my shoes. It was in this section that my feet began their steady descent to hell. The pain was concentrated on the balls of my feet, and unbeknownst to me at the time, was caused by massive blisters forming underneath the calluses. After a final leukotape patch job to stall further blister damage near the top of the climb, the mission was clear: make it to the campsite and get water. I put my head down and knocked out the last of the climbing and made it to a spot called Little Pine Flat, just off the Forest Service road that the AZT would follow for nine miles the following morning. There I found a stagnant pool of water filled with dead bugs, algae, and other miscellaneous debris. Straining the water, with the assistance of the bug head net, required patience and a steady hand, but eventually, I had 3 liters of treated water. Finally, I made my way to the site where I’d set up for the night, and it revealed itself as a gorgeous pine grove tucked in a nook of the mountain. Little Pine Flat was a bit of a new experience for me as I arrived two hours prior to sunset. I didn’t know what to do with all the time, so I did all the chores you could think of and still had time to do a full stretching routine before the sun was down. Because I had plenty of time before I needed to sleep, I set up the full tent to act as a windbreak and created my humble abode for the evening. With the chores done and a cozy home erected, sleep was the only thing that I needed to do. And boy did I do it well. The totals for day three: 26.3 miles (0.0 off-trail); total time: 11:45:13; moving time: 8:52:34; and elevation gain: 4,658 ft.

I woke up on day four after the best night of sleep that I’ve ever had on trail. Did my feet hurt? Sure. But I was ready to go. And thanks to my efforts the night before, I didn’t have any chores to worry about in the morning, so I packed up my gear and started walking at 5:40 am. Breakfast in bed has certainly been hyped up by both big breakfast and big bed; however, there is very little chatter on breakfast on the move at sunrise. And I think that is a tragedy. Walk in my shoes for a second. I have my trail breakfast in hand as I’m walking on a Forest Service road that is weaving amongst the mountain tops as the sky is illuminated in a light pink shade. I take a bite of my world-famous Chia Bomb and listen to the birds ring in the new day. In between bites, I noticed the duality of the light in the high mountains as some cut like a knife through the gaps, and other places were totally enveloped in the shadows of a nearby peak. Another bite went down, and I noticed the Four Peaks Wilderness Area ahead. It confused me a bit because for the longest time, I could only see three peaks. As the breakfast dwindled to nothing, I got glimpses of Roosevelt Lake, thirty miles south of my location, the destination for the day. Alright, I think you get the picture. It was an incredible start to the morning with massive views everywhere and easy walking.

That Forest Road that I have been raving about kept on for nine miles to start the day. With the net downhill trajectory and lack of technical footing, I felt like I was flying. It wasn’t too long before I passed the first hikers of the morning. They were just waking up and getting a later start to the day, so I gave the man standing outside the tent a silent nod and continued on with my morning. Walking on the Forest Service road was easy; the foot pain wasn’t being exacerbated by rocky, technical terrain, the views went on forever, and the conditions were perfect. As I was ambling along, I saw a familiar face. I asked, “Wait, aren’t you the bike guy?” Much to his chagrin, a charming British man confidently responded that he was indeed the “Bike Guy.” So, who is the bike guy, and how do I know the bike guy? The bike guy is Greg McMahon, a thru-hiker turned long-distance cyclist, who is currently cycling from the top of Alaska to the bottom of South America, and he puts out semi-regular updates on YouTube that can rack up views in the mid-hundred thousands range. So, when I saw Greg with a backpack on in the mountains of Arizona, I was a bit confused. He explained that his bike is currently in Ecuador, but he needed a reprieve from the rainy season, and he wanted to hike, so he came to Arizona to hike up to the Grand Canyon. Once he hits the Colorado River, he’ll begin the journey of getting back to Ecuador. We talked for maybe five or ten minutes, and once again, the interaction turned into a memory in the blink of an eye. My time on the Forest Service road couldn’t last forever, and off in the distance, a fork in the road indicated that the last minutes in this part of the journey were here. A car passed me right at the end, it didn’t ruin the immersion or anything, but it couldn’t have waited a few minutes until I was out of sight? Isn’t the universe supposed to be looking out for me specifically? The trail split from the road, and I reflected on an incredible morning and some of my favorite miles of trail that I had ever experienced.

I met a Dutch couple that didn’t speak great English right after the trail split, but they were very nice people, and you could tell that they were loving their experience of the trail and the trail community. The route moving off the road and meeting this couple marked the end of the first part of the day. I mentioned this in passing a bit earlier, but there was a clear-cut goal for the day: get to the Roosevelt Lake marina, a 30+ mile day. To get there, I broke up the day into three chunks: the Forest Service road, the Four Peaks Wilderness Area, and the descent to the marina. With the Four Peaks Wilderness Area dead ahead, I prepared myself for what I had built up to be the hardest part of the day. Turns out, I was right to build up the difficulty of this section. Here’s a little fun fact on federally designated wilderness areas: the trails are typically rougher than those that fall outside of this designation. There are a few reasons why: mountain bikes are not permitted in these areas, there are legal provisions against the use of motorized vehicles and mechanized tools, and a natural conditions directive that calls for trails to be “a bit more wild and less park-like.” Zooming back to the beginning of the Four Peaks Wilderness Area, the trail was almost immediately overgrown. And even though I wasn’t climbing thousands of feet, I still had to force myself through the spiky undergrowth while chugging uphill. By the time I was at the top of the climb, my fate for the next few hours was sealed, a choppy section with rocky footing that looked like a sawtooth when looking at the elevation profile. I’d be breaking through the undergrowth and making constant, but small, climbs and descents on the sides of mountains. Earlier in my hiking career, I would’ve let this string of annoying circumstances get in my head and bring down my mood; however, I have experience and can draw on the wisdom from either myself or from those that I have met on prior trips. Only a few miles into the Four Peaks Wilderness Area, I tapped into a conversation that I had with Pedestrian Crossing at Little Rock Pond in Vermont. I had come into the shelter all frustrated with the condition of the trail just north of the isolated pond in the Green Mountains, and was airing out my grievances. Pedestrian Crossing patiently listened, and once I wrapped up, he offered a simple message, “Sometimes, you have to meet the trail where it is and let it come to you. There is no reason to fight it, embrace it.” After feeling like a real punk, I thanked him for his outlook on the trail and vowed to remember that lesson when the going got hard.

So, I took a break and reset. I wasn’t about to let this beautiful section of trail be lost due to my frustrations with the conditions. Immediately, I started looking around and seeing the place with new eyes. The Four Peaks dominated the skyline above with the white, cliffy peaks darkening the blue skies in the background. Looking the other direction was Roosevelt Lake, stretching out over the land of sand and snakes far below. Sure, there were still trees here, but far fewer than in the Mazatzal Mountains. That didn’t mean that the mountains were any less green, just different. Beautiful in their own way. The miles in this stretch were far slower than the first part of the day, but with my renewed perspective, I saw it as more time spent in a beautiful section of trail. I met two other hikers in this section of trail, a woman named Trail Brain and a man who witnessed my futile efforts at managing blisters, but it still felt like a solitary fortress being tucked up in those mountains. Still battling through overgrown trail, I had my sights set on a water cache at mile twenty-one on the day. The guidebook comments stated that there was plenty of water left, so I took my chances and made a beeline for the sweet nectar of the Gods, easy, clean water in the desert. On my way down to the cache, I exited the Four Peaks Wilderness Area and was promptly swarmed by bugs. Until next time, Four Peaks.

As the elevation dropped sharply on the descent, I reminded myself to stop and take a look at the view behind me. Sure enough, the Four Peaks were shining in the midday sun, standing over me like a gentle guardian. Now came the tough part of the day, I had a 4,800-foot descent over eleven and a half miles. Sounds like it should be easy, right? Yeah. But the balls of my feet were, as the kids would say, “down bad,” and descents were the secret killer. So, I had finished what I thought would be the hardest part of the day, but the reality is that the real challenge had finally arrived. Just before 2 pm, I arrived at the aforementioned water cache just after mile twenty-one on the day. The feet were immediately released from their torture devices after sucking down one and a half liters as I crawled into the only bit of shade I could find. Loaded up with two liters of water, I shouldered the pack and continued the descent. Within a mile, I noticed something tall rising from the desert floor below. Before too long, it became clear that I was looking at my first saguaro cactus. My entire life, I’d seen this caricature in movies, car wash logos, and popular media, but seeing it for the first time in person scrambled my East Coast brain. I took note of the elevation, 3,200 feet, and carried on down the trail. The trail gods decided that novelty was needed because within five minutes, I encountered my first rattlesnake of the trip, spread out on trail in front of me. At around six or seven feet, homeboy was big. Our slithery friend didn’t seem too mean, so I made a wide circle off-trail and left him to soak up the mid-day sun.

Compared to the early part of the day, I might as well have been on a different planet. Pine groves were traded for saguaro forests and sharp, rocky peaks for red cliffs. The reminders of a different world still hung above me with the Four Peaks clear as day in one direction and the Superstition Mountains rising in front of me to the south. Smack dab in between was Roosevelt Lake, drawing ever closer, hour by hour, step by step. The Tonto Basin lay far below despite having spent a good deal of time descending as the realities of the Sonoran Desert became more clear, very, very little water. Still, I continued on, chipping away at the descent, until I arrived at a point where Lake Roosevelt was obscured by the hills surrounding the lake. My feet were sending very mean messages to my brain, begging to be done for the day, but the destination was now only a few miles away. After the second-to-last mini-climb of the day, Roosevelt Lake’s deep blue waters sat below, stretching out in every direction. There was one final steep, rocky descent down to the bridge to cross just before the Theodore Roosevelt Dam. On that descent, I met my final two hikers of the day, Dimebag and Dottie. These two were a phenomenal pairing that had met on the Pacific Northwest Trail the prior year when Dimebag hosted Dottie, an Australian thru-hiker, at her house. During that stay, they made plans to do a section of the Arizona Trail with one another the following year, and here they stood arm-in-arm with one another in the Sonoran Desert. One of the biggest pieces of news coming out of this conversation was that the restaurant at the marina was open and the portions were generous. With a little extra motivation, I motored through the rest of the descent, crossed the Salt River via a beautiful bridge that sat above the run-off point of Roosevelt Lake, and started the countdown until I had some air conditioning and 5,000+ calories. In one final gut punch, the trail climbed over 500 feet from the road, and I had to navigate an extra mile or two until descending once more to the marina below. In what did not feel like a blink of an eye at the time, I reached the turn-off point from the AZT and walked into the marina at 5:45 pm.

I didn’t make many mistakes on this trip, but one of them was that I omitted salty foods from my food plan and did not pack any electrolytes, so when I walked into the convenience store at the marina, I looked for the saltiest food I could find. Pringles it was. Straight down the hatch it went. Now, it was time for the real performance at Ffinches (no, I didn’t mistype, two Fs). I found a table in the corner to minimize the impact of my stench on the locals and plugged my phone into the outlet while I waited to order. When I plugged in my phone, I noticed that I was still around 45% and smiled a bit, knowing that I had just walked over 110 miles and had barely used my phone. The waitress came over, and I probably horrified her with the amount of food that I ordered, but the growing tip amount had her coming back and asking if I needed more; the answer was almost always yes. So, with a bowling ball in my stomach and the phone back to 100%, the task at hand was to find a place to sleep for the night. Nestled just beyond a parking lot at the marina was a flat patch of desert that was my little slice of heaven for the evening. My pack exploded on the ground around me, and I opted for a cowboy camping setup (no shelter, no bug net) as I settled into my little dirt patch for the night. I played a low-stakes game of “Operation” on my foot blisters, heavily relying on alcohol prep pads and hand sanitizer to ward off infection, called Liv to check in, and stripped down before climbing into the sleeping quilt for the night. In hindsight, maybe I should’ve planned better, because locals partying, domestic arguments, and road noise kept me on edge for the duration of the evening, but I was dead tired, and sleep couldn’t come fast enough. The totals for day four were 30.6 miles (0.4 off-trail); total time: 11:57:44; moving time: 10:29:06; elevation gain: 4,704 ft.

The morning of day five started with a dazzling slow sunrise over Lake Roosevelt and was topped off with a luxurious visit to the nearest porta-potty. With the early morning business and chores tackled, I recounted the mantra of the trip, the result being inevitable through perseverance, as I patched up the last of the blisters that had aired out overnight. The reason for recounting the mantra at mile zero was largely due to the fact that the day ahead was going to be challenging. Before even setting off, I knew that I had a boatload of climbing throughout the day, difficult footing through the upcoming Superstition Mountains, limited water, and isolated sections on trail. Oh, and the cherry on top, it was going to be the hottest day of the trip. With countless layers of suffering promised for the duration of the day, I was surprisingly not bothered. Sure, my feet were going to hurt, but I just told myself to put the foot pain in a box and tuck it away until after the trip. It seemed to work well enough for the past two days, so why not put it to the test once more? Away I went. But wait, I was missing something… a hand cover for the sun. I contemplated carrying one for the remainder of the trip, but decided that sunscreen would be more than enough for the day, so I tossed the sun hand cover. Upon reaching the trail junction with the AZT, I looked down, and almost glimmering in front of me was the missing sun glove. An intense mental battle began as to whether I should return to the marina trash can to recover the previously discarded sun cover or accept my loss and carry on. I decided that half a mile was a bit too far for that piece of gear, so I continued southbound, with one less sun glove. With a loss that great, it was really hard to continue (~s~).

The first stretch of the day was the climb out of Roosevelt Lake and back into the mountains. That climb was 3,000 feet over nine miles, nothing Earth-shattering, but still pretty noticeable. The morning started with me being surrounded by saguaros as I worked my way up to a Forest Service road, and it was shortly thereafter that the morning threatened to take a turn. The mileage and the elevation gain alone weren’t too bad; the main problem was the Cottonwood Creek wash. Similar to the Four Peaks Wilderness Area, this section of trail, though uniquely beautiful, was overgrown, and it was extremely difficult to get into any semblance of a rhythm. The wash resembled a maze with cairns, built at the height of all the other rocks strewn about, poorly charting the course ahead. Early on in the maze, I passed a group of older thru-hikers who were confused why I was hiking in my underwear, but were absolutely thrilled to hear that the food down at the marina was delicious. They looked as frazzled as I did, so I knew the upcoming miles were not going to be easy. My intuition was correct, I bobbed and weaved through the overhanging brush, taking my battle wounds from the sharp plants in stride, as I continuously crossed the dry creek bed searching for the rock piles within the rock piles. When the wash came to an end at mile six on the day, I left it with a little bird over my shoulder and carried on. Am I proud of what I did? No, but it was cathartic.

Without missing a beat, the grade of the climb sharply increased, and the next stop was a solar-powered water tank right at the border of the Superstition Wilderness Area. Once again, I kicked it into low gear and powered my way up the climb. When I got to the tank, I snapped a picture of the dead bugs floating on the surface of the shiny brown water before collecting said water and taking on the final mile of the climb. Just after saying my prayers to the Giardia Gods, I met a thru-hiker who we’ll call Jake. After just a few minutes of chatting, it came out that Jake was pretty lonely. The answer confused me a bit since I had been passing so many hikers each day, so naturally, I probed. Jake mentioned that he had only seen six thru-hikers total since starting the trail at the Mexican border over two and a half weeks prior. Shocked, I gave him the good news that there was a large bubble just ahead and that his experience might change sooner than he thinks. I wished him luck on the journey (and for the gross water just ahead) and continued the final part of the ascent. Reaching the top of the climb didn’t turn out to be an isolated peak with massive views everywhere; it was just another ridgeline in a sea of mountains. But it was the spiritual ending of phase one of the day, so I celebrated in silence in one of the most remote sections of the Superstition Wilderness.

Now, phase two of the day enters the scene. And this part had been fear-mongered for nearly one hundred miles. The tales of awful footing and constant, steep climbs were omnipresent on the trail. “Just you wait till you get to the Superstitions, there are some real climbs in there.” “Have fun sliding down the mountains in the Supes.” “Watch out for the bears in there.” Just a constant barrage of dire warnings. So, standing at the gateway, I asked myself, “How bad could this possibly be?” and carried on. I had a few miles to mentally prepare, but in reality, I just kept my brain as empty as possible. I marveled at the mountainsides covered in purple from the wildflower bloom, I watched as the birds floated effortlessly overhead, and gawked at the rock formations on the mountaintops all around. All of a sudden, I was at the base of a pretty large descent and had the first of three notable climbs in front of me. Onward and upward. An hour later, the first climb was done. On the descent, I made a mental note of how the Superstition Wilderness had a different feel relative to the Mazatzals or Four Peaks; the terrain was shrubby and desert-like with mountains that sported rocky outcroppings via ancient volcanic eruptions. Trees were off in the distance, but they just didn’t look the same as those in other ranges. It was clear that I was getting further south. By the time that this thought had cycled through my mind, I was at the base of the steepest climb of the bunch, the one that had been endlessly mentioned by the northbound thru-hikers who had to come down the other way. There was a good enough reason as to why it was infamous. I gained 900 feet in under a mile, but after twenty-five minutes of steep climbing, it was done. Maybe the southbound experience was far different than my northbound counterparts, but I was finding the Superstitions to be fun, not overly difficult. The third climb came and went without too much fanfare, just more climbing and descending, and before I knew it, phase two of the day was done and dusted. The gnarly part of the Superstitions that had been the stuff of legends for the past hundred miles was now in the rear view, and the only thing that separated me from the finish line was twenty-five miles.

Reavis Ranch, an oasis in the Superstition Wilderness, caught me totally off guard. This part of the mountain range was a world removed from the brushy, dusty sections of the northeastern portion of the wilderness area. A nondescript Reavis Creek marks the invisible border where you are transported into a world unlike the rest of the surrounding area. There was a moment while I was sitting at the creek when I remembered being extremely confused at the stark transition. There was a sycamore just in front of me and a whole meadow of knee-high green grass, and it threw my brain into a tizzy. How was this there? Why was it there? How could you have these desert peaks formed through ancient volcanoes rising above such a lush place? Was there a point in contemplating, or should I just embrace it and enjoy it? I stopped overthinking, crossed the creek, and started my leisurely stroll through the old ranch site. Pretty much immediately, I ran into the first deer I had seen my entire time out there. Sure enough, I ended up seeing five more over the few miles that the trail crossed over the old homestead site. I did not see any of the infamous apple trees that apparently still drop apples each fall, but maybe next time. With one last scoop of water from Reavis Creek, I exited the oasis and climbed back to reality. My time spent in the Reavis Ranch area was far too short for my liking, and I’d love to revisit in the future. This part of the Arizona Trail was my second favorite of the trip, only trailing the Mazatzal Wilderness.

Upon my reentry to reality, I was faced with the problem of water scarcity once again. I had a guaranteed dry camp ahead, and I had roughly another ten miles until the next reliable water source in the morning. So, that meant your boy needed to carry some water. Four liters was the consensus that the group (mind, body, and spirit) came to, so once I reached a nasty, brown puddle, I gathered the four liters, hoisted the pack, and continued the march. Everything was going swimmingly until, less than half a mile from the wilderness area border, I had a code brown emergency. Think DEFCON 2, teetering on the edge of DEFCON 1. Like a racecar pulling into pit lane, I found the flattest spot in the immediate vicinity and promptly returned to DEFCON 5. Crisis averted. I imagine my relief was similar to that of JFK’s after the Cuban Missile Crisis. A few pounds lighter, I crossed out of the Superstition Wilderness and began the hunt for a proper campsite. I walked through sunset for the first time in the trip and settled on a dodgy site just off a Forest Service road. It was pretty hard-packed, but it was flat and right on trail, so it was good enough after a long day in the saddle. The nightly ritual of unpacking, setting up the sleep system, and completing the list of chores/stretches began under the moon rising in the sky above. I settled into the site and reminisced on a job well done: 27.1 miles (0.4 off-trail); total time: 13:08:54; moving time: 10:54:23; elevation gain: 7,328 ft.

Before Day Five could officially be put in the books, there was one final surprise: vehicles. No longer asleep in my bug bivy, I noticed a high-pitched whizzing noise in the distance. Still a bit delirious from being woken up at around 10 pm, it took me a little while before I could find the zipper to look out. That little gap in time was all it took for a UTV travelling over 60 miles per hour on the Forest Service road to cut through my peace and quiet like a knife. Being only ten feet from the road itself, I started to fear a potential return and the probability of a really bad outcome with a person acting so recklessly. The same cycle of indecision and anxiety that I have faced a few too many times forced its ugly reality into my mind, but this time I made a calculated decision to stay calm and keep my ears open for the high-pitched whizz. When I could no longer hear the vehicle, I pulled out my phone, inspected the likely route that they would take, and made the determination that I was probably safe to sleep. So, I did. About an hour later, bright headlights hit the trees above my head. “Oh no.” This time, it was an SUV moving safely and slowly, so as long as they didn’t pull into the campsite and crush me, I was safe. The car moved on, and I remained in my non-squished form. Once more, it was bedtime, and this time, for the remainder of the night.

Now, Day Six can officially enter the fold. Based on the determination the evening before, the alarm on my watch blared before 5 am, signaling for me to get my ass up to the top of the mountain for the sunrise. Only one and a half miles from the top of the saddle, I started in the dark after a quick pack-up. The trail followed a Forest Service road to the saddle, so no headlamp was needed as just enough light called out the rocks and rutted tracks underfoot. Before too long, I was on the ridgeline and off to the east was a gorgeous desert sunrise. Leading up to the trip, I had heard so many people rave about the unbelievable sunrises and sunsets in the desert, and for whatever reason, I had not had my “ah-ha” moment. That changed as the sky raged a fiery orange on the eastern horizon and the 360° view opened up to a sea of mountains near and far, latching onto the first light of the day. It was a cosmic gift hand-delivered to me on my last day on the AZT. As the sunrise was replaced by long shadows and the full influence of daylight, I reached the gate on top of the saddle and snapped a picture of the Arizona Trail icon with a sea of mountains framed by the gorgeous morning sky.

After the short ascent was over, I then began the eleven-mile, 3,200-foot descent. The first few miles were steeper and lined with tall, dry grass that hung over the trail. The walking was fairly straightforward, but I had the thought of bikers being overwhelmed by the grass grabbing on their arms and legs, threatening to pull them down. From my mouth to God’s ear, I immediately passed a couple cowboy camping near the top of the climb with the bikes standing guard as sentinels over the likely exhausted cyclists. Ah, it is possible, and I am probably just weak-minded, great! I followed their tire tracks down the steeper portion of the descent until I met two more thru-hikers. They were younger, and maybe I was just a bit burnt out of these short conversations, but my heart just wasn’t in this conversation. I told them that the Superstitions were fun, Reavis Ranch is a gem, and the Mazatzals will steal their heart, and wished them well on their northward journey. I followed their footsteps for the next few miles, periodically dipping in and out of a wash. In one of my now common excursions into the wash, I found a puddle of water that allowed me to drink what I had been carrying and fill the bottles anew. Thirst quenched, and five miles down, I pushed forward. It was only a few miles later that I came to the Reavis Canyon Trailhead that sported an informational guide and a trail register. I opened the book and reminisced on all the folks that I had met on the trip, and right in front of me was a piece of their present experience etched into history. Wild Child, Paiute, Porcupine, Chickweed, Speedy, Pineapple Bitch, Kevin, etc., all had signed the trail register in the days prior to my arrival. With each name, I was transported back to our conversation and the landscape that surrounded us at the time. I probably spent ten minutes just reliving my journey through the names on the page. After my brief stint in time travel mode, I myself grabbed the pen and, for the first time, wrote down “Ghost.”

At this point in the day, I found myself back in the land of the saguaros. The tree-like cacti were all around, and rose on the mountains all around me. I wove through red rock canyons and open desert terrain, and I began feeling the emotions of the ending more than the pain in my feet. The ending seemed too close. Too real. There was nothing more that I wanted other than more time on trail. Luckily, I still had a few more hours on trail to cling on to, and that meant that I still had time to experience more moments that would turn into memories. I had three final hits in the last ten miles of my journey. First, a bizarre saguaro cactus with arms wildly swinging about in every direction. Offshoots spun out of the offshoots of the offshoots. It looked a bit like a kindergartener got a little ambitious with his idea of a cactus. Only five minutes later, I got the second hit, a gila monster. What is a gila monster? Well, the gila monster, aside from the really cool name, is the largest lizard native to the United States, measuring up to 22 inches long with a stout, black body covered in bead-like, pink, orange, or yellow markings. Gila monsters are very venomous, but because they are so slow-moving, they are very rarely a threat to humans. The reptile is found in the southwestern U.S. and Mexican deserts, and spotting one can be fairly rare as they spend ~90% of their time underground. Luckily, I was able to witness one waddling along on trail in front of me. I thought I gave it a respectable amount of distance; however, it voiced its displeasure with me with a sinister hiss. I said goodbye to my angry new friend and left it to do its gila monster things. Seeing a highly venomous lizard the size of a small dog was a pretty new experience for me, and after I walked, stupidly grinning, at my luck for seeing one in the wild. The third and final notable moment came when I was less than five miles from the end. I ran into my final two hikers on trail, Steven and Ice Cold Bitch. Their initial vibe gave off feelings of tremendous experience, so I probed. Starting with Ice Cold Bitch, going by “Icy,” she is a Texan thru-hiker who has completed the PCT and CDT, and her tan, muscled legs showed that she meant business. Steven, on the other hand, was a bit heavier, and athleticism wasn’t an attribute that screamed out upon first glance. What popped out was the closed-cell foam sleeping pad that was incredibly worn down. I knew he meant business. And boy did he. Apparently, Steven has been hiking for two and a half straight years and has hiked almost every big trail in North America. When I asked what he did this past winter, he told me that he went and hiked through the Andes in Chile. My man is legit. With a wealth of knowledge in front of me, I asked for their best advice for long-distance hikes in the desert. Steven chimed in immediately, “Grow hooves and get used to water that looks like chocolate milk.” After a few more minutes, they congratulated me on finishing the section, and I wished them well on their journey.

All day, a large mountain off in the distance was slowly reeled in. That mountain was Picketpost, an ancient volcano, and unbeknownst to me at the time, it marked the end of my journey. Minute by minute, I drew closer to the inevitable. I spent a lot of my time during those last few miles deep within my own head. The feelings were complicated. I was thrilled to have finished the section hike, but I was sad that my one major trip for the year was over. I felt strong as an ox, but my feet felt like they were going to fall off at any moment. I loved the Arizona Trail, but I missed the Appalachian Mountains. And that was one of the most unexpected consequences of my time in Arizona. I really missed the green tunnel and began to appreciate my home environment more. Without a doubt, the open terrain of the west is objectively more beautiful, but there is something about the embrace of a forest that I love dearly. As my brain ping ponged around ten different competing thoughts, I opened and closed gates denoting the AZT and wondered whether the trail was asking me if I was sure that I was finished. Each time, I reaffirmed that I was sure, and the end came into view. I reached the Picketpost trailhead at 12:41 pm and officially ended the 159-mile section hike in five days, three hours, and thirty-nine minutes. As with all of my section hikes to date, there was no celebration. Just a quiet sense of accomplishment, and that’s enough for me. The totals for the sixth and final day: 17.2 miles; total time: 07:21:36; moving time: 06:03:18; elevation gain: 1,235 ft.

Although the walking portion of the trip had ended, there was one final hurrah in store: the trail angel’s house. Within five minutes of finishing, the trail angel, MJ, was there to pick me up in her SUV that sported purple angel wings on the doors. She gave me a quick rundown of the local history on the drive to Superior, which included geology, indigenous history, and the complicated mining history in the area. I asked MJ about Los Hermanos, but she directed me to Jalepanos instead, another local Mexican restaurant. There I ate two enchiladas, rice and beans, and the best burrito I’ve had in my life. I talked to the waitress, Crystal, and she said that the secret was a house-made chili con queso that filled the insides of the burrito. At some point, I put the shovel (my fork) down and looked around the restaurant. Sitting across from me was an older couple; the husband’s health was obviously poor as his hands shook while putting on his “Fuck Cancer” hat. They asked what I was up to, and I explained my trip and how I had just finished about twenty minutes prior and needed a burrito. I’m not sure these folks wanted to know, but they got a full crash course on the long-distance hiking community and how their town was a crucial point on the trail to act as a temporary pit stop within the Sonoran Desert. The conversation meandered through all different parts of life, and we both enjoyed one another’s company. Sufficiently stuffed, I got up to pay, and Cyrstal, the waitress, kindly explained that the older couple had already covered my meal. Immediately, I turned to the couple and protested profusely. My resistance was no match for their generosity, so in the end, I thanked them and exited their lives forever.

After taking down the best burrito of my life and experiencing overwhelming generosity, I walked to MJ’s house, scrubbed myself down in the shower, and threw on some loaner clothes before starting laundry. There, I spent time with four other hikers: Keagan (a PCT thru hiker who is running the Moab 240), Wet Fruit (Keagan’s girlfriend on her first thru hike and business partner for their guiding company Wayward Adventures), Shephard (a German Lufthansa pilot), and Washer (a retired rancher who recently thru hiked the PCT). We played silly games, talked about the trail, and opened up about our respective lives. Nobody was on their phone, and we were simply embracing the present moment, enamored by the diversity of lifestyles tucked into this small house in the desert. MJ, the trail angel, gifted us pizza dough and we had a pizza party all together that evening. As hiker midnight approached, we all wished one another goodnight and went to sleep. Shephard and I manned the bunk beds, and as the young buck, I got the top bunk. The next morning, we awoke to the smells of a home-cooked breakfast radiating from the kitchen. As we stumbled out of our respective beds, the piles of blueberry pancakes emerged only to be flanked by a massive dish of egg casserole. We promptly proceeded to devour the food, laughing at every bite along the way. Within the hour, the hiking outfits were back on, and we were out, back on our own paths. Washer, Keagan, and Wet Fruit continued north on the AZT, and Shephard and I climbed into MJ’s car, bound for the Phoenix airport. I was dropped off at 10 am and bid farewell to MJ and Shephard. After waiting for twelve hours in the airport, I boarded my overnight flight to New York City, where I actually fell asleep (a rarity for me on planes). I landed the next morning at JFK and took the subway back to Midtown Manhattan, where I opened up the laptop and returned to the “real world.” The journey was over.

What do I want to leave you with? When you find something that you love. And you can't have it all the time. It is insanely valuable to be hyper-present in the moment. There are so many distractions that you could let slide in, especially in our day and age. It's so easy to let outside forces influence the moment. But when you have that thing that you truly love and when you're getting to do it, do not let minor inconveniences or boredom or pains or whatever distract you from the fact that you are living in that moment. And I think that the more that you can be present in the moment, the more you can actually live the life that you want to live and to be satisfied with the life that you are living.

Remember to value your friends and loved ones. Don’t be afraid to be bored. Believe in the inevitability of a task when the perseverance, abilities, and purpose are aligned. Maintain a positive attitude through difficult situations. And be confident in the experiences you have lived, but be humble enough to ask questions and learn from those who may be more experienced. Most of all, have fun, let it rip, and go live the life you want to live.
